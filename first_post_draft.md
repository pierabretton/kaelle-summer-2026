# The Holy Crap Reality of Finding Summer Camps for a 3-Year-Old in London (and Raising a Trilingual Kid Without Family Support)

**The Bottom Line Up Front:** Finding summer camps in London for a 3-to-4-year-old that actually build social skills and burn energy is a massive challenge. After testing multiple options, we found the best mix for Kaelle: Kensington Ballet School (for structure), Dakodas (for performing arts variety), Teddy Tennis (for high-energy fun), and NWG Gymnastics. We’re also testing Kalmer Kids for mindfulness. If you’re a working parent in London without local family support, this is the survival guide you need.

---

Raising a kid in London is not easy. When you grow up in Latin America, there is an entire ecosystem of family support built into the culture. Grandparents, aunts, and cousins are often just around the corner, ready to help with childcare. In London, that village simply doesn’t exist in the same way. You have to build it yourself. 

Add to that the challenge of raising a trilingual kid—balancing English, Spanish, and another language—and the mental load becomes immense. We want Kaelle to be fluent, confident, and culturally connected, but that requires constant, intentional effort. 

The "Holy Crap" reality is that working full-time while ensuring your child gets the right social interaction and stimulation is exhausting. Lately, building social skills has been more difficult for Kaelle. She has an incredible amount of energy and needs structured environments—sports, puzzles, books—to channel it positively. That’s why finding the right summer camps became our lifeline. 

Camps aren’t just childcare so we can keep working; they are essential spaces for Kaelle to explore new activities, interact with other kids, and discover what she truly enjoys. 

Here is exactly how we built our summer survival calendar, the camps we trust, and why they work for a high-energy three-year-old.

## 1. The Strict (But Loving) Structure: Kensington Ballet School

Kaelle joined the Kensington Ballet School when she was just 18 months old. Miss Rachel has been amazing for her. She teaches manners, discipline, and the fundamentals of movement. 

Miss Rachel is a bit strict, which is actually fantastic for Kaelle’s personality. It provides the clear boundaries she needs while still being a joyful, creative environment. If your child needs help with focus and following instructions in a group setting, this is the place.

## 2. The Variety Pack: Dakodas Performing Arts Camp

When Kaelle turned three, we enrolled her in Dakodas. The brilliance of Dakodas is the variety. Instead of one long, monotonous day, the sessions are broken down. We were able to put her in three 30-minute classes back-to-back: ballet, gym, and drama. 

This rapid transition between activities is perfect for a three-year-old’s attention span. It keeps her engaged, moving, and constantly trying new ways to express herself.

## 3. The Energy Burner: Teddy Tennis

Kaelle started Teddy Tennis at age three, and she literally loves it. It’s high-energy, fast-paced, and incredibly social. 

What started as a fun activity has turned into a core part of her routine. She now has two friends from school who join her once a week. Kaelle usually goes twice a week because she needs that intense physical output. If you have a child who bounces off the walls, Teddy Tennis is the perfect outlet. 

## 4. The Olympic Dream: NWG Gymnastics

This is a recent addition. Kaelle has always wanted to do "Olympic gymnastics," but most serious programs require kids to be at least three and a half to start. 

We recently joined NWG Gym, and she is slowly warming up to it. It’s a different kind of discipline compared to ballet or tennis—more focused on core strength and bravery. So far, it’s going well, and we’re excited to see how she develops.

## 5. The Mindfulness Test: Kalmer Kids

This one came highly recommended by one of the mums at Kaelle’s school. Kalmer Kids focuses on nature play, mindfulness, and creative calm. 

Given how high-energy Kaelle’s other activities are, we wanted to introduce something that helps her slow down and regulate her emotions. We will be trying this camp out and will report back on how a high-octane toddler handles mindfulness!

## The Unexpected Gift: The Parent Village

One of the most profound things we’ve realized on this journey is that the parents of the other kids are friends that your kids gift to you. 

When you don’t have family nearby, these other parents become your support network. We share the same challenges—the logistical nightmares, the tantrums, the exhaustion—and we celebrate the same small victories. We are so happy for that support. 

## Why We Stayed in London (The Gleneagles Pivot)

I originally wanted to go somewhere cooler for the summer. I found Gleneagles in Scotland—they have an absolutely amazing family camp. 

However, my mum couldn’t come to London, and my husband just started a new job, meaning he couldn’t travel at the moment. The reality of traveling alone with a three-year-old was just too daunting. 

So, we decided to stay in London. To make up for missing out on the Scottish golf experience, a friend recommended Dukes Meadows Golf in Chiswick. They have a fantastic junior golf program, so we’ve added that to our London summer survival guide instead!
